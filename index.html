Before UJ: Where My Story Started

HOME TOWN

Raised by community, not just by one house.

My hometown taught me to work hard, stay respectful, and never waste an opportunity. Long before I knew what Food Technology was, I knew I wanted a life that made my family proud.

EARLY CHILDHOOD

Curious from the beginning.

I was always the kid asking why things work the way they do. Growing up, I learned to see small routines as lessons in patience, consistency, and discipline.

SCHOOL YEARS

Primary built habits. High school built ambition.

Primary school gave me foundations and confidence. High school sharpened my focus and made me believe I could compete in bigger spaces.

DREAMS AND AMBITION

I wanted a career with impact, not just a qualification.

I did not want to study just to pass. I wanted knowledge that could become real solutions, real products, and real value in the food industry.

That is the version of me who arrived at UJ: hopeful, underprepared in some ways, but fully committed to growing into the woman I knew I could become.

The Grind Years

Second year taught me discipline. Third year taught me resilience. Final year taught me that burnout is real, and rest is part of the work. I learned to ask for help, to study smarter, and to stop tying my worth to one bad grade.

I had nights where I cried over deadlines, mornings where I questioned everything, and afternoons where one good lecture restored my confidence. Progress looked messy, but it was still progress.

Some lecturers challenged me hard and refused to let me shrink. They corrected my reports, questioned my assumptions, and taught me how to think like a professional, not just a student.

Five Years, One Food Technology Journey

Year 1

Foundation in Food Science

I stepped into UJ's Food Technology world with equal parts nerves and curiosity. Year one built my base in chemistry, food safety, and the discipline required for lab work.

Year 2

Lab Confidence and Consistency

This was the year of practicals, process discipline, and precision. I learned to trust my methods, ask better questions, and treat every experiment like a step toward mastery.

Year 3

Applied Projects and Real Pressure

Modules got harder, expectations got higher, and deadlines got tighter. I started connecting theory to production realities and learned that consistency beats panic every single time.

Year 4

Completing the Diploma

The final diploma year tested everything at once: technical depth, time management, and resilience. I crossed the finish line with a full Diploma in Food Technology and a bigger belief in myself.

Year 5

Advanced Diploma in Food Technology

I stayed for one more year to sharpen my specialization. The advanced diploma pushed me into deeper technical thinking and gave me the confidence to step into the industry with real expertise.

The Semester That Almost Broke Me

There was one semester where nothing clicked. My confidence crashed, deadlines piled up, and I started doubting whether I belonged here at all.

Tests felt heavier than usual, one exam result shook me, and I had to rebuild my routine from scratch.

What changed everything was not one dramatic moment. It was a series of small decisions: asking questions in class, planning my weeks, going to consultations, and trusting that effort would eventually compound.

I stopped chasing perfect. I started chasing consistent.

Proof That It Was Worth It

  • Academics
  • Extracurriculars
  • Campus Life
  • Survival Skills

Somehow managed to keep my GPA up while simultaneously leading societies and working part-time. My secret? Copious amounts of coffee and the fear of disappointing my parents.

My final capstone focused on food quality, safety, and process discipline in product development. Defending it to the panel was terrifying, but hearing "well done" from people I respect is a moment I will never forget.

I supported junior students with practical prep and lab confidence. Explaining methods to someone else made my own understanding deeper and more disciplined.

Presented product ideas and process thinking with my peers. Learning to communicate technical work to different audiences prepared me for life after university.

First year res taught me patience, how to cook two-minute noodles in a kettle, and that noise-canceling headphones are the greatest invention of the 21st century.

I am now fully certified to write a 3,000-word essay using only the flashlight on my phone and a laptop running on 12% battery. The ultimate extreme sport.

A self-taught skill I hope to never use again. Highly proficient in walking to 8 AM exams looking like a zombie. Character building at its finest.

Graduation Day, Finally

The moment they called my name, everything slowed down. I thought about first-year nerves, difficult semesters, and all the days I wanted to quit.

Then I crossed the stage, held my degree, and felt this quiet sentence in my chest: you did it, and you did it honestly.
