In The Lab: Where Theory Becomes Real

Lab sessions were my favorite part of university. Lectures teach concepts; labs teach discipline. They taught me that good science is 10% inspiration and 90% meticulous execution.

Titrations & Standardization

Technique: Acid-base titration with precision to 0.05mL

Started with wobbly burette hands. Ended with results reproducible to 0.1%. This taught me that precision comes from patience and practice, not talent.

Spectroscopy & Analysis

Technique: UV-Vis, HPLC, and data interpretation

Learning to read spectra taught me to think in data. Every peak tells a story if you know how to listen. Connected abstract chemistry concepts to measurable reality.

Food Preservation Research

Technique: Shelf-life testing and microbial analysis

My capstone project. Testing preservation methods on actual food products. Failed batches taught me more than successful ones—because I had to understand why.

The Shift

When I Stopped Being A Student

It wasn't graduation day. It wasn't the final exam results. It was a moment in the lab, halfway through my third year, when a batch of food preservation samples came back with unexpected results.

My first instinct was panic: "Did I make a mistake?" But then I stopped. I looked at the data again. And instead of seeing failure, I saw a question: "Why did this happen?"

That simple shift—from "what went wrong?" to "why did this happen?"—changed how I approached every challenge after that. It's the difference between troubleshooting and understanding. Between following procedures and thinking like a scientist.

My lecturer saw me re-running tests to understand the variance. She smiled and said: "Now you're doing real science." That moment, more than any grade, told me I had become what I came to university to become.

What University Taught Me

Precision Matters

The difference between a 68% yield and 72% might seem small. But in food safety or medicine, that 4% could be critical. I learned to care about decimal places.

Failures Teach More Than Wins

Every failed experiment taught me more than every successful one. Because I had to understand what went wrong and how to correct it next time.

Real Mentorship Changes Everything

I didn't just learn chemistry from my lecturers. I learned how to be someone who cares about their work and their students. That's the real legacy.

Think Deeper, Dig Longer

University taught me that the first answer is rarely the best answer. Most growth happens when you push past the surface-level response.

Walking across the graduation stage wasn't the end of my university story. It was the beginning of the part where everything I learned actually matters in the real world. And that's where the real journey begins.
